  <div class="description_body"><p><strong><em>The Waste Land</em>, Annotated</strong></p>

<p><img src="http://images.rapgenius.com/1688d8d05b80a850c2551387e31c7421.460x385x1.jpg" height="385" data-animated="false" width="460" alt=""></p>

<p>T.S. Eliot’s mind-bending, language-blending retort to the stripped-down simplicity of the <a rel="nofollow" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imagism">Imagists.</a> Much of the meaning in <em>The Waste Land</em> is derived from the allusions and name-dropping peppered heavily throughout the poem. The style is often described as “fragmented” or collage-like. This kind of fragmentation, as variously practiced by Eliot, Ezra Pound, Marianne Moore, and others, was one of the defining features of <a rel="nofollow" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernist_poetry">Modernist poetry</a> in English.</p>

<p>Inspired in part by James Joyce’s <a rel="nofollow" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulysses"><em>Ulysses</em></a>, which evokes a day in the life of a Dublin man-about-town through an elaborate series of parallels with Homer’s <em>Odyssey</em>, Eliot wanted to write a poem structured around an even more timeless story: that of the <a rel="nofollow" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetation_deity">vegetation deity,</a> which dies and reincarnates according to the seasons and their associated fertility. Eliot applies this scheme to modern life, with “fertility” corresponding roughly to “sexual passion” or “lust for life.”</p>
